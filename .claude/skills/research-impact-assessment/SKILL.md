# Research Impact Assessment

## Core Rule

Assess impact as a multidimensional contribution. Do not use journal impact
factor, venue prestige, or raw citation count as a proxy for article quality.

## Impact Dimensions

Evaluate only dimensions relevant to the user's question:

- Scholarly influence: citations, replication, extensions, debate, method reuse,
  data/software reuse, and position in the field.
- Methodological contribution: new measures, designs, datasets, analytic tools,
  protocols, or transparent reproducible resources.
- Clinical/public-health/educational impact: guideline uptake, practice change,
  implementation evidence, effect on patient/student/community outcomes.
- Policy/social impact: citations in policy, standards, law, public agencies,
  advocacy, public discourse, or institutional decisions.
- Translational/commercial impact: patents, products, clinical trials,
  deployment, interoperability, or standards adoption.
- Equity and relevance: whether benefits, harms, and applicability are assessed
  across populations and settings.

## Workflow

1. Define the impact question.
   - Impact for whom, in what domain, over what time window, and compared with
     what alternatives?
   - Separate novelty, quality, influence, and usefulness.

2. Gather indicators.
   - Prefer field-normalized citation indicators over raw counts when available.
   - Use citations as evidence of attention or influence, not correctness.
   - Include non-citation evidence: guidelines, policy documents, systematic
     reviews, clinical trial lineage, software/data reuse, media only when relevant.

3. Contextualize indicators.
   - Account for field, publication year, article type, language, database
     coverage, team size, open-access status, and citation lag.
   - Identify negative citations, controversy, replication failures, and retracted
     or corrected work.

4. Link impact to evidence quality.
   - Use quality appraisal separately before claiming "high impact and reliable."
   - Flag influential but weak, preliminary, or methodologically limited studies.
   - Flag rigorous studies with low citation counts when the field is new, small,
     local, or practice-oriented.

5. Write the assessment.
   - State the strongest evidence of impact.
   - State limitations of the indicators.
   - Distinguish demonstrated impact from plausible future significance.

## Output Pattern

- Impact question and time window.
- Evidence sources used.
- Scholarly impact indicators.
- Practice/policy/translation indicators.
- Quality and credibility caveats.
- Balanced impact judgment.
- Missing data or follow-up needed.

## Guardrails

- Do not rank researchers, institutions, or grant proposals from one metric.
- Do not use journal impact factor to judge individual article quality.
- Do not treat altmetric attention as endorsement or scientific validity.
- Do not compare raw citation counts across fields or publication years.
- Do not ignore harm, controversy, corrections, or failed replication.

## Standards To Anchor

Use DORA and the Leiden Manifesto for responsible metrics, NIH iCite/RCR or
field-normalized indicators when appropriate, and narrative impact logic when
policy, clinical, educational, or community impact matters more than citations.
